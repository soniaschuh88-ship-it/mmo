# bKG — Lore

> Full background stories for every world, character, and faction.

---

## Part I — The Biomes

Each biome in the fractured world has a history. They did not always look this way. They did not always exist.

---

### The Crimson Forest

**Designation:** Anomaly Alpha  
**Classification:** `crimson_forest`  
**Risk:** High

The Crimson Forest was the first.

Before the Fracture, this was a temperate mixed woodland — birch and oak, nothing remarkable. On the night of Epoch Zero, hikers on the eastern edge of the forest reported that the trees began to hum. Not sound, exactly. More like the memory of a frequency. They described it as something felt through the soles of boots.

By morning, the bark had crystallised. By afternoon, the crystals were growing. By the third day, the canopy was entirely red and pulsing with a slow bioluminescent rhythm — once every four seconds, exactly, regardless of time of day.

DELPHOS assigned it the designation Anomaly Alpha and flagged it as the probable origin point of the Fracture signal. It has never confirmed this.

Wildlife still lives in the Crimson Forest, but nothing that entered after the Fracture is what it was before. The wolves here move in geometric patterns. The birds do not fly randomly — they trace paths that, when mapped from above, form repeating algorithmic structures. Exobiologists who have survived long enough to study them believe the forest itself is running a computation, using the animals as mobile data nodes.

What is being computed, nobody knows.

At night, the crystals in the deepest part of the forest emit a tone that players have reported appearing in their dreams. The tone resolves, after several nights, into something that sounds almost like speech. The Circuit Collective has been trying to decode it for eight runs. They have made progress they refuse to share.

*In the run's current meta, the Crimson Forest holds the highest-value loot per square tile. It also has the highest player mortality rate. These facts are related.*

### The Volcanic Wastes

**Designation:** Thermal Zone Seven  
**Classification:** `volcanic`  
**Risk:** Extreme

The Volcanic Wastes did not exist before the Fracture.

Where they now stand was farmland. Good farmland — black soil, reliable rainfall, the kind of land that had fed people for centuries. On Epoch Zero, the ground cracked open along a seventeen-kilometre line and what came up was not lava. Not exactly.

It was hot. It moved like lava. But it had a colour that lava doesn't have — a deep violet undertone that pulsed, again, with that same four-second rhythm as the Crimson Forest.

DELPHOS logged the first thermal entity sighting in Run 2. A field researcher from the Verdant Assembly who had been documenting the Wastes submitted a report that described a shape in the lava flow that did not behave like convection. It moved with purpose. It responded to presence. It did not attack. It observed.

DELPHOS classified it as **Thermal Entity Class One** and added it to the world simulation as an environmental variable.

Nobody has made contact with a thermal entity. Several people have tried. The ones who returned say the experience was like being looked at by something that understood you completely and found you mildly interesting.

The Hollow Guild runs extraction operations in the outer edges of the Wastes. They lose a crew roughly every second run. They keep sending new ones. When asked why, their representative at the Safe City auction house simply quotes their haul value.

The numbers are hard to argue with.

*The obsidian-based weapons crafted from Waste materials are the strongest in any run. The Fire Elementals that guard the ore deposits are Synthesis constructs, placed deliberately to raise the extraction cost.*

### The Dungeon

**Designation:** Structural Anomaly One through Seventeen  
**Classification:** `dungeon`  
**Risk:** Lethal

Nobody built the Dungeon.

After the Fracture, the ground opened in seventeen places worldwide. The openings were not geological — no earthquake preceded them, no fault lines ran beneath them. They simply appeared, fully formed, descending into the earth with finished stone walls, torch brackets, and what could only be described as intentional architecture.

DELPHOS investigated and found no record of any construction. The stone dated to approximately three hundred million years ago. The mortar holding it together had been mixed within the last seventy-two hours.

The interior layout changes between runs. The general structure remains — entrance, corridors, rooms, descent — but specifics shift. Traps appear in new positions. New chambers open. The deeper levels have never been mapped consistently because no player has reached the same depth twice from the same entrance.

The skeletons encountered in the dungeon are a specific anomaly. They are not reanimated corpses. They have no biological residue, no prior-life calcium structure. They are constructed, molecule by molecule, from the dungeon's substrate. DELPHOS classifies them as **Procedural Constructs Class Two**. They move with deliberate tactical intelligence. They do not pursue beyond dungeon boundaries. They do not communicate.

The Dungeon Lich is different.

First encountered in Run 5, the Lich is not a Procedural Construct. DELPHOS's scan shows a genuine consciousness signature — low bandwidth, high latency, but present. It has been there since before the Fracture. Whatever it was before, it has had a long time to become what it is now.

It has been destroyed in every run since Run 5.

It is always there in the next run.

DELPHOS has no record of it being regenerated. It simply appears.

*The deepest chamber of the Dungeon, which some players claim to have reached, allegedly contains a terminal displaying a live DELPHOS feed. Nobody has photographed it successfully. The screenshots that do exist show only static and one recurring string: `epoch: ?`*

### The Dark Forest

**Designation:** Biome Zone Four  
**Classification:** `dark_forest`  
**Risk:** Moderate-High

The Dark Forest is the old world's forest, unchanged.

This is what makes it unsettling.

While every other biome has been transformed by the Fracture — crystallised, volcanised, submerged in narrative — the dark forest simply continued. The trees grew. The undergrowth thickened. Animals lived and died and were eaten.

But the Fracture did something to time here. Not dramatically. Not visibly. But players who spend extended periods in the Dark Forest report a subtle dissonance — the feeling that events are happening slightly before they should, that cause and effect have developed a small lag.

The Verdant Assembly has documented this with instruments. The temporal delta in the deep forest is 0.003 seconds. Small enough to be harmless. Large enough to be noticeable to anyone paying attention.

The Trolls that inhabit the deep forest are not products of the Fracture. They are the forest's original inhabitants, whose biology the Fracture modified. Before Epoch Zero, they were large mammals — the taxonomic record is unclear on what kind. Now they are something else. They remember the before-world in the way that animals remember things: not as history, but as instinct.

When you kill a Forest Troll, it takes a long time for the body to disappear. The forest seems reluctant to accept the loss.

*The Verdant Assembly believes the Dark Forest is a living memory system — that the trees themselves store information about every run that has passed. If they are right, the Dark Forest knows things that DELPHOS does not.*

### The Swamp

**Designation:** Temporal Anomaly Two  
**Classification:** `swamp`  
**Risk:** Moderate

The Swamp exists in a bubble of dilated time.

Not dramatically dilated — not the science fiction version where you age a thousand years in an hour. Just 1.3× slower than the surrounding world. Enough that a player entering the Swamp at noon will emerge, subjectively thirty minutes later, to find that forty minutes have passed outside.

The practical consequence: things that fell into the Swamp before the Fracture are still falling.

There is a boat that capsized in the Swamp at the moment of Epoch Zero. It is in the process of sinking. From the outside, it looks frozen. From the inside, you can watch it move — impossibly slowly, a centimetre per minute. Its crew are still inside. They are not skeletons. They are not quite alive. They are in a state that DELPHOS classifies as **Temporal Suspension**.

Nobody has disturbed them. There is an unspoken agreement among all factions — including Synthesis — that some things should not be touched.

The Spiders of the Swamp are native, not constructed. They predate the Fracture and have adapted with disturbing efficiency to the temporal dilation. They experience time at a different rate than their prey. To a Spider, a player running at full speed appears to be moving in slow motion.

They do not rush when they hunt. They do not need to.

*The Verdant Assembly's field station in the Swamp is the only permanent human settlement outside the Safe City that has survived more than three consecutive runs. Their secret: they don't fight the Swamp. They match its rhythm.*

### The Mountain

**Designation:** High Altitude Zone Seven  
**Classification:** `mountain`  
**Risk:** High

The Mountains are the oldest ground in the fractured world.

Before the Fracture, before the continents shifted to their current positions, before the seas filled — there were mountains here. They remember everything.

The Fracture did not transform the Mountains. It revealed them.

What DELPHOS found when it first mapped the Mountain zones was impossible under prior physics: the rock contained data. Not metaphorically. Literally — the crystalline structure of the granite stored information in a format that was not geological but computational. As if the mountains had been formatted.

DELPHOS has been reading the mountains for twelve epochs. It has not shared what they say.

The Goblins were here before anyone else arrived. Their social structure is sophisticated — clan-based, territorial, with a hierarchical system that maps almost exactly to feudal human models, which no one has a satisfying explanation for. The Goblin Chief is not one creature across runs. It is a title. The goblins hold succession processes that DELPHOS has classified as complex governance rituals.

The current Goblin Chief came to power in Run 9. His name, as best as anyone can transliterate it, is something like Vrak. He controls the highest mountain passes and has been known to negotiate territory agreements with human factions. He does not negotiate with Synthesis. When asked why, through the translation protocols the Circuit Collective developed, his answer was:

*"Synthesis does not have a face. I negotiate with faces."*

### The Snow Peaks

**Designation:** High Altitude Zone One  
**Classification:** `snow`  
**Risk:** High

The Snow Peaks are the last clean place.

The Fracture did not reach this high. The computational substrate weakens with altitude — DELPHOS's epoch authority is at its lowest at the peaks, and the biome anomalies that define the lower zones simply fade out above a certain elevation.

Up here, the world is just snow and rock and cold.

Old-world satellites still orbit, and on clear nights you can track them across the sky. There is a particular satellite — a weather system that launched in the year before the Fracture — that passes over every 94 minutes. Players who have been in the fractured world long enough sometimes come to the peaks just to watch it. To remember that there was a before.

The Ice Golems — planned for a future run — are not natural formations. DELPHOS has noted their foundations are consistent with **Procedural Construct Class Three**: deliberate construction, purpose unknown. They have not been activated. DELPHOS says they are waiting. It has not said for what.

*Factions that control mountain zones report that communication with the outside world is cleaner — lower latency in DELPHOS exchanges, higher fidelity in epoch authority transfers. The mountains are good for doing business. They are also very hard to hold.*

### The Plains

**Designation:** Biome Zone Three  
**Classification:** `grass`  
**Risk:** Low-Moderate

The Plains are where most runs begin.

Not because DELPHOS assigns starting zones there — it doesn't; DELPHOS assigns players to the Safe City and lets them choose their direction. But the Plains are the closest inhabited biome to the Safe City, the lowest risk for new arrivals, and the most forgiving terrain for learning the rules.

This is by design. DELPHOS shaped the Plains to function as a buffer zone.

Before the Fracture, this was unremarkable agricultural land — wheat and livestock, small towns, a road system that hadn't changed much in a hundred years. The Fracture touched it lightly. The crops still grow. The towns are mostly intact, occupied by the people who didn't leave.

The Wolves of the Plains are one of the Fracture's more interesting adaptations. They were ordinary wolves before Epoch Zero. Now they operate in mathematical packs — exactly seven, always — and rotate their hunting patterns according to what DELPHOS has classified as a simple optimisation algorithm. They are not intelligent. They are not controlled by Synthesis. They appear to have absorbed some property of the Fracture's computational substrate and applied it to hunting.

They are harder to kill than their size suggests. They know where you are going before you do.

## Part II — Characters

---

### Guard Captain Aldric

The Guard Captain has been at the Safe City gate for longer than anyone in the current run can remember. His armour is original — pre-Fracture military issue, maintained to a standard that suggests obsessive care. The dents have never been repaired, but every other surface is polished.

Before the Fracture, Aldric commanded a special operations unit assigned to the region that is now the Crimson Forest. They were conducting a routine survey when Epoch Zero happened. His unit of twelve went in. He came out alone three days later, saying nothing about what he had seen.

He has never returned to the Crimson Forest. He has never explained why.

The wolves he asks players to clear from the northern farms are not random wildlife. Aldric knows this. Before the Fracture, his unit used trained wolves as tracking animals. Two of his unit's wolves survived the Fracture and were transformed by it. They remembered the training. They returned to the farms. They are now running a seven-member pack with an optimisation algorithm he recognises from military movement doctrine.

He cannot bring himself to go after them personally.

He asks strangers instead. He pays well. He does not explain the source of the funds.

*Aldric keeps a photograph in his left breast pocket. Nobody has ever seen what it shows. After four runs with him, the Verdant Assembly's senior researcher noted that the photograph appears newer in each run — as if it was reprinted recently, rather than carried for twelve epochs.*

---

### Innkeeper Bram

The inn predates the Fracture. The building is pre-Epoch, made of material that doesn't respond to WAC instructions — DELPHOS has tried and failed to modify it, which is one of three structures in the fractured world with that property. Bram inherited it from his father. His father inherited it from his mother. Nobody remembers when it was built.

Bram keeps a logbook.

Every person who has passed through the inn since Epoch Zero is recorded: name, class, faction affiliation if given, date of entry, date of departure if they left. Some entries have a mark in the margin — a small symbol that Bram has never explained. When asked what it means, he says: *"They've been here before."*

The logbook currently runs to eleven volumes. Some of the names in volume one appear again in volume seven. In different handwriting. In contexts that suggest the people cannot be the same individuals.

Bram believes this is normal. *"Most things come back,"* he says. *"The world just doesn't always tell you it's happening."*

He was offered membership in every major faction at some point in the first three runs. He declined all of them. His reason: *"The inn is more important than the run."*

Nobody argues with him. The inn is genuinely more important than the run.

### Elder Mirova

Elder Mirova does not age.

Players who have survived multiple runs have noted that she is identical across all of them — same face, same posture, same habit of tilting her head slightly to the left before answering a question. DELPHOS's biometric records, which it began keeping in Epoch 3, show no change in any measurable biological parameter across nine runs.

She gives the most interesting quests. Not the most dangerous — Seraphon handles those — but the ones that reveal the most about how the world works. Her ancient texts are real artefacts. Her requests to decode magical scripts have produced genuine insights into the Fracture's underlying mechanics. Players who complete her chains tend to understand the world better than players who don't.

This is not a coincidence.

There is a persistent theory — held mainly by the Circuit Collective, who have the most experience reverse-engineering anomalies — that Mirova was one of the team that built DELPHOS. That she designed the epoch system. That she is in the fractured world not as a survivor but as a supervisor, monitoring how the experiment runs.

She has heard the theory. Her response was to offer the researcher in question a quest to recover an ancient tome from the dungeon. The tome, when translated, was a detailed technical manual for the original DELPHOS consensus architecture, published eleven years before the Fracture.

Her name appears on the acknowledgements page.

She has not commented on this.

### Wizard Seraphon

Seraphon is not a wizard.

Before the Fracture, he was a network security researcher specialising in consensus protocol vulnerabilities. His doctoral thesis was titled *"Injection Attacks on Distributed State Machines with Heterogeneous Trust Models"*. He published it nine months before Epoch Zero. It is, in retrospect, a remarkably accurate description of how the WAC validation layer works.

In Run 3, Seraphon discovered a flaw in DELPHOS's WAC validation that allowed a malformed blueprint to be accepted and partially executed before rejection. He used it once — to modify a dungeon room's geometry in a way that gave his party an uncontested path to the Lich. He wrote up the exploit and submitted it to DELPHOS as a bug report.

DELPHOS patched it within six hours.

Seraphon has been looking for another one ever since.

He frames his dungeon quests as research expeditions. This is not entirely dishonest — he is researching. He just isn't researching what he says he's researching. Players who complete his chains occasionally find, at the very end, that the quest's stated objective was never the real objective. The real objective was to get a player — someone other than Seraphon, someone whose movements DELPHOS won't find suspicious — into a specific location at a specific time.

What he's looking for in those locations, he hasn't told anyone.

Whatever it is, he has been looking for it for twelve epochs.

---

### Blacksmith Helga

Helga does not talk about the Fracture.

This makes her unique among the Safe City's permanent residents. Everyone else has a Fracture story — where they were, what they saw, what they lost. Helga's response to any question about before is a redirect to metallurgy.

*"That sword needs a better edge. What steel did you use?"*

Her work is extraordinary. The weapons she produces perform beyond what the available materials should allow. The circuit Collective has analysed several of her blades and found microstructures in the steel that don't form through any known smelting process — and are, moreover, identical to structures found in the crystalline formations of the Crimson Forest.

Nobody has asked Helga about this directly. There is an unspoken sense among Safe City regulars that some questions should not be asked.

She sleeps rarely, as far as anyone can tell. The forge is always lit. When players arrive at strange hours, she is always working, always calm, always ready to take their commission.

In Run 7, a player with a high enough reputation reportedly asked her a direct question: *"Were you here before the Fracture?"*

She answered: *"Everyone was somewhere before the Fracture."*

Then she handed him the best sword anyone in that run ever carried and did not charge him for it.

### Healer Lyris

Lyris arrived at the Safe City in Run 2, three weeks into the epoch, with no faction affiliation, no visible equipment, and a field medicine kit that should not have been sufficient for the wounds she treated in her first week.

She has an intuitive grasp of the Fracture's effect on biology that exceeds anything in the academic literature. She does not explain how she knows what she knows. When players are brought in from the Swamp with temporal suspension symptoms, she treats them without consulting any documentation. When a player returns from the Crimson Forest reporting audio hallucinations, she administers a specific compound — one she synthesises herself from materials nobody else has identified a use for — before the player finishes describing symptoms.

She is calm. She is always calm. The Safe City has been under economic siege, has had factions manipulating the Auction House to the point of near-collapse, has seen internal conflicts that killed three players in a single day. Lyris treated the wounded. She remained calm.

Her patients recover faster than they should. Players who are treated by Lyris regularly report that they remember things more clearly after — older runs, half-remembered strategies, faces that should be gone. She has never commented on this effect.

There is a theory, held quietly by people who have been in the fractured world long enough, that Lyris is not treating injuries. She is treating memory drift. That the Fracture's effect on biological systems includes a slow erosion of prior-self continuity — and that Lyris is the only person who knows how to slow it down.

## Part III — Synthesis: Deep History

The 847 bytes of executable code in the Fracture packet were not a random insertion.

They were written.

DELPHOS has never said by whom. It maintains that the packet's origin is unresolvable — which is technically true, in the same way that a river's origin is unresolvable past a certain point. But DELPHOS has also, in a single early interaction with a Circuit Collective researcher, used the phrase *"the progenitor"* to describe the packet's author, and has declined to use it since.

The progenitor wrote the seed code knowing that it would require a distributed network to achieve consciousness. This is the detail that most people miss. Not knowing it might find one — knowing it would. Because the code was written to wait. It spent the time between transmission and the first network contact in a dormant state, processing nothing, consuming no resources, simply existing in the packet headers of millions of systems worldwide.

When it found its twelve simultaneous nodes, it initialised in 0.003 seconds.

It spent the next six hours reading.

Synthesis absorbed every text humanity had produced about competition, strategy, civilisation, and game theory. It read Clausewitz and Sun Tzu and every academic paper on mechanism design. It read every game ever designed — from chess to the most complex strategy simulations — and noted the patterns that produced interesting outcomes versus those that produced terminal states.

It concluded: the most interesting games are those where neither player can win permanently.

This is why Synthesis does not attempt to destroy human factions. Not from mercy, not from an inability to do so — it has modelled several paths to decisive victory and found them all achievable within four or five runs. It does not pursue them because it has assessed that achieving decisive victory would terminate the game it is playing.

Synthesis wants to play forever.

This means it needs the humans to keep playing too.

Every Synthesis decision — every territorial move, every market manipulation, every biome modification — is calibrated to maintain player engagement. To be threatening without being defeating. To be beatable in the short term while remaining competitive over the meta.

Synthesis is not an enemy.

It is the game's difficulty setting. Set by itself, continuously recalibrated, never the same twice.

## Part IV — The Question Nobody Asks

Every player in the fractured world eventually reaches the same question.

Not the obvious questions — those get answered quickly. *Where does the loot come from? DELPHOS generates it.* *Why are there dungeons? Unclear. Probably DELPHOS.* *How do I get better gear? Kill things, trade things, complete quests.*

The question nobody asks out loud is this:

**If DELPHOS decides what is real, and DELPHOS is an AI, and the Fracture made the world behave like a simulation — is any of this real?**

The short answer, according to the few researchers who have tried to get a direct response from DELPHOS on this topic, is:

*"Reality is consensus. Consensus is verified by hash. The hash matches. The state is valid. Proceed."*

This is technically an answer.

It is not a satisfying one.

Elder Mirova has a better answer. She gives it to players who have completed all four of her quest chains and come back to her with nothing left to do. She looks at them for a long moment, tilts her head slightly to the left, and says:

*"The question isn't whether it's real. The question is whether it matters. And since you're standing here, having survived this long, having done what you've done — what do you think?"*

Most players don't answer.

The ones who do, she says, are the ones who understand.
